What Stayed With Me

Morning Fog

Some mornings do not ask us to travel anywhere. They simply ask us to look outside.

Front Porch→Stories→Morning Fog

The morning began like so many others.

After leaving a job that had consumed nearly every hour of my day, I had slowly begun rebuilding something I had not realized I had lost: time.

Instead of rushing out the door, mornings became an opportunity to pause. One of the first things I would do was look through the window to understand the day waiting outside. Would it be warm, foggy, or windy? It became a quiet ritual of paying attention before everything else began.

That morning, something felt different.

The dense fog that had settled over the Carquinez Strait was beginning to lift, but it had not disappeared. Warm light filtered through it in a way I had never seen before, giving the bridge an almost impossible glow.

I stopped where I was. My camera was only a few steps away, resting on the kitchen table from the evening before. I reached for it, stepped through the front door, and returned to the porch before the light had finished changing.

Some mornings change our plans. Others remind us why it is worth slowing down enough to have plans of our own.

What Stayed With Me

I did not remember the fog first. I remembered stopping.

For years, my mornings belonged to schedules, deadlines, and the next obligation waiting beyond the front door. That morning reminded me that paying attention is not something we fit into whatever time remains.

The bridge did not become beautiful because I had more time. I finally had enough time to notice it.

What might become visible if you reclaimed the first few minutes of your own morning?
