What Stayed With Me

The Oak Remembers

The oldest thing we can see is not always the oldest story a place has to tell.

Front Porch→Stories→The Oak Remembers

I was not planning an afternoon of exploration.

I had driven my father to one of his recurring eye appointments in Walnut Creek. The visits often lasted long enough that sitting in the parking lot never felt like the best use of the time. So I looked at a map.

Less than five miles away was a place I had never visited: Walnut Creek Open Space.

What I found surprised me. Rolling grasslands stretched through an otherwise highly developed part of Contra Costa County, interrupted by magnificent California oaks that appeared completely at home despite the city growing around them.

Then I learned something that changed the scale of the landscape.

Beneath those old trees were the remnants of something much older: shells embedded at Shell Ridge, evidence that this land once rested beneath an ancient sea.

Standing beneath the branches, I could not stop thinking about time. The oaks felt ancient. The ocean quietly reminded me that they were newcomers.

Sometimes a place teaches history without saying a single word.

What Stayed With Me

I left thinking about perspective.

It is easy to assume that the oldest thing visible is the oldest story the land contains. Sometimes a landscape asks us to look beneath the surface.

The trees invited me to look upward. The hillside invited me to look back millions of years. Both belonged to the same place.

What older story might be resting beneath a landscape you think you already know?
